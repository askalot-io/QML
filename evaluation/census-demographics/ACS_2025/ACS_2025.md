# American Community Survey 2025: Declarative Conversion Analysis

**Source:** U.S. Census Bureau, American Community Survey, Form ACS-1(INFO)(2025), 48 pages
**QML Files:** `evaluation/reference-questionnaires/ACS_2025/`
**Date:** 2026-03-29

## Objective

Transform the American Community Survey 2025 paper questionnaire (48 pages, arrow-based skip routing) into a declarative QML representation, then run the Z3-based formal validator to detect structural properties and design patterns in the original.

## Methodology

1. **PDF preprocessing**: Text extracted via `pdftotext -layout` to `ACS_2025_ocr.md` (1,359 lines)
2. **Question inventory**: 140 items catalogued across 12 sections, including 13 lettered filter boxes (A-M) and 45 skip routing decisions
3. **Declarative QML conversion**: 11 section files with preconditions replacing imperative skip arrows. Filters converted to block-level and item-level preconditions. Two consolidation variables (`worked_last_week`, `worked_past_5_years`) used for employment path routing.
4. **Formal validation**: Z3 SMT solver run on all 11 files at validation level 2 (per-item classification, global satisfiability, dependency loops, path-based reachability)

## Problems in the Original ACS 2025 (Exposed by Declarative Conversion)

### P1: Tiered Disability Age Gates Create Non-Obvious Coverage Gaps

**Severity:** LOW
**PDF evidence:** Pages 13-14, Filters H and I

**Problem:** The disability questions use three different age thresholds:
- Q18a-b (hearing, vision): No age restriction — asked of all persons
- Q19a-c (cognitive, walking, dressing): Age >= 5 (Filter H)
- Q20 (independent living / errands): Age >= 15 (Filter I)

This creates three disability coverage tiers that are not made explicit to the respondent:
- **Ages 0-4**: Only hearing and vision disability captured
- **Ages 5-14**: Hearing, vision, cognitive, ambulatory, and self-care disability captured
- **Ages 15+**: All six disability domains captured

**In the imperative version:** The tiered gates are spread across two pages with separate filter boxes (H and I). A respondent filling out the form may not realize that younger household members receive truncated disability screening.

**In the declarative version:** The three block-level preconditions (`person_age >= 0` implicit, `person_age >= 5`, `person_age >= 15`) make the tiered structure immediately visible in the QML. Z3 classifies all items in the age-gated blocks as CONDITIONAL, confirming the gates are mathematically distinct.

### P2: "Occupied Without Payment of Rent" Produces No Housing Cost Data

**Severity:** LOW
**PDF evidence:** Page 10, Q20 option 4; Pages 10-11, Filters B and C

**Problem:** When a household selects Q20 option 4 ("Occupied without payment of rent"), the form instructs them to skip to Filter C, which then skips to Filter E. This means they bypass:
- Q21a-b (monthly rent)
- Q22-Q27 (property value, taxes, insurance, mortgage, mobile home costs)

These households contribute zero housing cost data to the survey. While this is intentionally designed (they neither rent nor own), the paper form's skip arrows make it hard to see that option 4 creates a complete data gap across 16 questions.

**In the imperative version:** The skip chain (`Q20=4 → Filter C → Filter E`) routes the respondent through two lettered filter boxes. The cumulative effect of skipping both renter and owner sections is not obvious from any single instruction.

**In the declarative version:** The QML makes this explicit through block-level preconditions: `b_renter` requires `outcome == 3` and `b_owner` requires `outcome in [1, 2]`. Option 4 satisfies neither, so the entire costs/tenure section is skipped. Z3 confirms these blocks are CONDITIONAL with no overlap with option 4.

### P3: Employment Section Has Highest Routing Complexity

**Severity:** LOW (observation, not defect)
**PDF evidence:** Pages 16-18, Q30-Q42 with Filters K, L, M

**Problem:** The employment section (27 items, 52 dependencies) accounts for 46% of the entire questionnaire's dependency graph (52/114 total). It uses 2 consolidation variables (`worked_last_week`, `worked_past_5_years`) and 6 blocks to model five distinct respondent paths:

1. **Currently working + drove** → work location + commute details + carpool + work history + employer
2. **Currently working + other transport** → work location + commute (no carpool) + work history + employer
3. **Currently working from home** → work location + work history + employer (no commute)
4. **Not working, recent** → job search chain + work history + employer
5. **Not working, long-term/never** → job search → income only

The job search sub-section (Q36a-Q38) has the most intricate branching: a three-way fork on layoff status (Q36a) feeds into recall (Q36c) or temporary absence (Q36b), each with different convergence points at Q37 (looking for work) and Q38 (availability).

**In the imperative version:** The five paths are expressed through 14 skip arrows scattered across three pages. The convergence points are implicit — following the arrows requires careful page-by-page tracing.

**In the declarative version:** Each path corresponds to a block with explicit preconditions. The Z3 solver verifies all 27 items are CONDITIONAL but reachable, confirming the routing has no dead code. The two consolidation variables cleanly separate the work-status classification (immediate: `worked_last_week`) from the historical classification (5-year window: `worked_past_5_years`).

### P4: Minimal Validation Constraints for a 40-Minute Questionnaire

**Severity:** MEDIUM
**PDF evidence:** Entire questionnaire (pages 1-18)

**Problem:** The ACS contains only 1 implicit validation constraint across 136 items: `bedrooms <= rooms` (Q6b <= Q6a). For a questionnaire estimated at 40 minutes of respondent time, the absence of cross-field validation is notable. Missing constraints that could improve data quality include:

- Q40b (weeks worked, 0-52) has no upper-bound enforcement relative to Q40a (worked every week → should be 52)
- Q24 (year last married) has no constraint relative to age (should be <= current year and >= age 15)
- Q44 (total income) has no constraint relative to the sum of Q43a-h (the form instructs "Add entries... subtract any losses" but cannot enforce this on paper)
- Q8_YEAR (year came to US) has no constraint relative to age or birth year
- Q26c (years responsible for grandchild) has no constraint relative to grandchild's age

**In the imperative version:** Paper forms cannot enforce cross-field validation — respondents self-correct or errors are caught during data processing. The Census Bureau presumably applies these edits during post-processing.

**In the declarative version:** These become expressible postconditions that Z3 can verify. The current QML models only the bedrooms <= rooms constraint (CONSTRAINING classification). Adding the others would shift from passive data collection to active validation. This represents the primary value that formal questionnaire verification provides over paper-based administration.

### P5: Housing Tenure Creates Three Mutually Exclusive Paths with Unequal Question Counts

**Severity:** LOW (observation)
**PDF evidence:** Pages 10-11, Q20, Filters B-E

**Problem:** Q20 (tenure) creates three respondent paths with very different question burdens:

| Tenure | Path | Questions | Page Span |
|--------|------|-----------|-----------|
| Owned (with mortgage) | Q22-Q27 | Up to 12 items | Pages 10-11 |
| Owned (free and clear) | Q22-Q27 | Up to 12 items (skip Q25b-d) | Pages 10-11 |
| Rented | Q21a-b | 2 items | Page 10 |
| Occupied without rent | None | 0 items | -- |

Homeowners with mortgages answer 6x more questions than renters in the Housing section. This burden asymmetry is inherent to the different data needs (mortgage details, property taxes, insurance) but creates a significantly longer experience for homeowners.

**In the declarative version:** The 6-block structure of `04_housing_costs_tenure.qml` (28 items total, 21 CONDITIONAL) makes this asymmetry immediately visible. The Z3 dependency graph shows `b_owner` is the largest block with 12 items forming a chain through Q25-Q26.

## Impact Assessment

The ACS 2025 is a mature, well-designed questionnaire from the U.S. Census Bureau. The declarative conversion reveals several notable properties:

**Structural quality**: The questionnaire has zero structural defects — no unreachable items, no infeasible constraints, no dependency cycles. This reflects decades of iteration and testing. The 13 lettered filter boxes (A-M) form a clean hierarchical routing structure with no cross-cutting dependencies.

**Routing concentration**: The employment section alone accounts for 46% of all dependencies (52/114), while the remaining 10 sections are relatively simple. This concentration means most of the survey's logical complexity is localized, making maintenance and modification easier.

**Paper form limitations**: The conversion highlights constraints that a paper form cannot enforce. The ACS relies on post-processing edits rather than inline validation. A digital-first version could add 5-10 postconditions (income totals, date consistency, age-dependent constraints) that would catch errors at the point of data collection.

**Extern variable pattern**: The cross-file dependency pattern is minimal — only `person_age`, `person_sex`, and `building_type` are shared across sections. This clean separation makes the questionnaire well-suited for modular administration (e.g., presenting Housing and Person sections independently).

## Conclusion

The ACS 2025 converts cleanly to declarative QML with 136 items across 11 files. Z3 formal verification confirms the questionnaire is structurally sound: all items are reachable, the global formula is satisfiable, and no dependency cycles exist. The five findings (P1-P5) are design observations rather than defects — they expose structural properties (tiered age gates, mutually exclusive tenure paths, complexity concentration, missing validation constraints) that are non-obvious in the paper form's imperative skip-arrow format but become immediately visible in the declarative representation. The primary value of the conversion is the identification of missing validation constraints (P4) that could improve data quality in a digital administration context.
